# AWS Well-Architected Framework — Cost Optimization Pillar

## Definition

The Cost Optimization pillar of the AWS Well-Architected Framework focuses on avoiding unnecessary costs and applying best practices for designing, delivering, and maintaining AWS workloads with cost efficiency. A cost-optimized workload fully utilizes all resources, achieves outcomes at the lowest possible price point, and meets the functional requirements of the business. This pillar guides you to architect workloads using the most effective mix of services, resources, and pricing models so that business outcomes are delivered at the lowest price point, while keeping continuous improvement and flexibility at the core. Its audience spans both technology roles (CTOs, architects, developers, operations) and finance roles (CFOs, controllers, financial planners, business analysts).

## Design Principles

1. **Implement Cloud Financial Management.** To achieve financial success and accelerate business value realization in the cloud, invest in Cloud Financial Management. The organization must dedicate the necessary time and resources to build capability in this new domain of technology and usage management — through knowledge building, programs, resources, and processes — similar to building Security or Operations capability.
2. **Adopt a consumption model.** Pay only for the computing resources you consume, and increase or decrease usage depending on business requirements. For example, development and test environments are typically only used for eight hours a day during the work week; stopping them outside of those hours can save approximately 75% (40 hours versus 168 hours).
3. **Measure overall efficiency.** Measure the business output of the workload and the costs associated with delivering it. Use this data to understand the gains made from increasing output, increasing functionality, and reducing cost.
4. **Stop spending money on undifferentiated heavy lifting.** AWS does the heavy lifting of data center operations like racking, stacking, and powering servers, and managed services remove the operational burden of operating systems and applications. This allows you to focus on customers and business projects rather than IT infrastructure.
5. **Analyze and attribute expenditure.** The cloud makes it easier to accurately identify the cost and usage of workloads, allowing transparent attribution of IT costs to revenue streams and individual workload owners. This helps measure return on investment (ROI) and gives workload owners the opportunity to optimize resources and reduce costs.

## Best Practice Areas

### 1. Practice Cloud Financial Management (CFM)

Covers the people, processes, and tools required to manage, optimize, and plan cloud spend. CFM rests on four sub-pillars: **See, Save, Plan, Run**. Treat cost optimization as a continuous discipline with shared accountability between finance and engineering, not a one-time exercise.

**COST01 — How do you implement cloud financial management?**
- Establish ownership of cost optimization with a named team or FinOps function (BP01).
- Establish a partnership between finance and technology so both speak the same units (BP02).
- Set cloud budgets and forecasts that account for variability and growth (BP03).
- Implement cost awareness in your software development lifecycle and organizational processes (BP04).
- Report and notify on cost optimization progress; monitor cost proactively with anomaly detection (BP05–BP06).
- Keep up to date with new AWS service releases and quantify the business value generated by cost optimization (BP07, BP09).
- Build a cost-aware culture through training, gamification, and incentives (BP08).

### 2. Expenditure and Usage Awareness

Covers governance of who can do what, monitoring cost and usage at the granularity needed to act, and decommissioning resources no longer required. Without visibility and guardrails, cloud's elasticity becomes unbounded spend.

**COST02 — How do you govern usage?**
- Develop usage policies aligned to organization requirements (BP01).
- Implement goals and targets for cost and usage (BP02).
- Implement an account structure (multi-account via AWS Organizations) that isolates workloads, environments, and business units (BP03).
- Implement groups and roles with least-privilege access (BP04).
- Implement cost controls (Budgets actions, SCPs, quotas, IAM boundaries) to prevent unexpected spend (BP05).
- Track project lifecycle so resources are tied to a project owner and end date (BP06).

**COST03 — How do you monitor your cost and usage?**
- Configure detailed information sources (Cost and Usage Report at hourly/resource granularity) (BP01).
- Add organization information to cost and usage via cost allocation tags and AWS Cost Categories (BP02).
- Identify cost attribution categories that map to business units, products, or environments (BP03).
- Establish organization metrics such as cost per customer, per transaction, or per feature (BP04).
- Configure billing and cost management tools (Cost Explorer, Budgets, Anomaly Detection, QuickSight on CUR) (BP05).
- Allocate costs based on workload metrics so engineering teams see unit economics (BP06).

**COST04 — How do you decommission resources?**
- Track resources over their lifetime via tags, CMDB, or Config (BP01).
- Implement a decommissioning process with formal handoff and approvals (BP02).
- Decommission resources actively when projects end or owners change (BP03).
- Decommission resources automatically with lifecycle policies, scheduled scripts, or event-driven Lambdas (BP04).
- Enforce data retention policies so storage does not grow indefinitely (BP05).

### 3. Cost-Effective Resources

Covers selecting the right service, the right resource type/size/number, the right pricing model, and planning for data transfer. The cheapest infrastructure is often a managed or serverless service that eliminates idle capacity.

**COST05 — How do you evaluate cost when you select services?**
- Identify organization requirements for cost (acceptable TCO, payback) (BP01).
- Analyze all components of the workload, not just compute (BP02).
- Perform a thorough analysis of each component, including managed vs. self-managed (BP03).
- Select software with cost-effective licensing, including BYOL options and open source (BP04).
- Select components to optimize cost in line with organization priorities (BP05).
- Perform cost analysis for different usage patterns over time (steady state, peak, growth) (BP06).

**COST06 — How do you meet cost targets when you select resource type, size, and number?**
- Perform cost modeling that maps configurations to expected workload (BP01).
- Select resource type, size, and number based on data — actual utilization, not guesses (BP02).
- Select resources automatically based on metrics (Auto Scaling, Compute Optimizer recommendations) (BP03).
- Consider using shared resources (multi-tenant, container platforms) where workloads tolerate it (BP04).

**COST07 — How do you use pricing models to reduce cost?**
- Perform pricing model analysis every 2 weeks to 1 month using Cost Explorer recommendations (BP01).
- Choose Regions based on cost where latency and data sovereignty allow (BP02).
- Select third-party agreements with cost-efficient terms; avoid vendors whose pricing scales with your full AWS bill without adding proportional value (BP03).
- Implement pricing models for all components — Savings Plans for compute, RIs for RDS/ElastiCache/OpenSearch/Redshift, Spot for stateless workloads (BP04).
- Perform pricing model analysis at the management account level so commitments aggregate across linked accounts (BP05).

**COST08 — How do you plan for data transfer charges?**
- Perform data transfer modeling — map flows between AZs, Regions, internet, and on-prem (BP01).
- Select components to optimize data transfer cost (CloudFront, VPC endpoints, Direct Connect, regional service placement) (BP02).
- Implement services that reduce data transfer cost (caching, compression, S3 Transfer Acceleration only when justified) (BP03).

### 4. Manage Demand and Supply Resources

Covers matching supply to demand so you do not pay for idle capacity. Demand can be shaped (throttled, buffered, queued) and supply can be made dynamic (scheduled, scaled, event-driven).

**COST09 — How do you manage demand and supply resources?**
- Perform an analysis on the workload demand to understand fixed vs. variable patterns and acceptable latency for change (BP01).
- Implement a buffer or throttle to manage demand (SQS, Kinesis, API Gateway throttling, rate limits) (BP02).
- Supply resources dynamically using Auto Scaling (target tracking, predictive), Lambda concurrency, Fargate, and scheduled scaling for known patterns (BP03).

### 5. Optimize Over Time

Covers reviewing the workload regularly against new AWS services, features, and pricing, and automating operations so optimization compounds rather than decays.

**COST10 — How do you evaluate new services?**
- Develop a workload review process that defines cadence, scope, and owners (BP01).
- Review and analyze the workload regularly against AWS What's New, re:Invent launches, and price reductions; capture findings as a backlog (BP02).

**COST11 — How do you evaluate the cost of effort?**
- Perform automation for operations — quantify the labor cost of manual tasks vs. building automation, and prioritize automating tasks with high recurrence or high error risk (BP01).

## Pricing Models Cheat Sheet

- **On-Demand:** Pay per second/hour with no commitment. Use for unpredictable, short-lived, or new workloads being benchmarked.
- **Savings Plans — Compute:** Up to ~66% off; flexible across EC2, Fargate, Lambda, any Region, any instance family. Default choice for variable compute footprints.
- **Savings Plans — EC2 Instance:** Up to ~72% off; locked to a specific family in a specific Region but flexible across size, OS, tenancy. Use for steady-state workloads where the family is stable.
- **Savings Plans — SageMaker:** Discounts on SageMaker instance usage for ML workloads with steady training/inference baseline.
- **Reserved Instances — Standard:** Highest discount (up to ~72%) for RDS, ElastiCache, OpenSearch, Redshift, DynamoDB reserved capacity, and MemoryDB. 1- or 3-year terms; limited modification.
- **Reserved Instances — Convertible:** Lower discount than Standard but exchangeable for different families/OS/tenancy; use when long-term shape is uncertain.
- **Spot Instances:** Up to ~90% off On-Demand for spare capacity; can be reclaimed with 2-minute notice. Use for fault-tolerant, stateless, or checkpointable workloads (batch, CI, big data, stateless web tier behind an LB).
- **On-Demand Capacity Reservations:** Reserve capacity in a specific AZ without a pricing commitment; combine with Savings Plans or RIs for capacity assurance plus discount. Use for events, DR, or compliance.

## Key AWS Services & Tools

**Visibility**
- AWS Cost Explorer — interactive cost/usage analysis and rightsizing/SP/RI recommendations.
- AWS Budgets — cost, usage, RI/SP coverage and utilization budgets with alerts and Budgets Actions.
- Cost and Usage Reports (CUR / CUR 2.0 in Data Exports) — hourly, resource-level billing data for QuickSight or Athena.
- AWS Cost Categories — group costs by business dimensions (team, product, environment).
- AWS Cost Anomaly Detection — ML-based detection of unusual spend with email/SNS notifications.

**Optimization**
- AWS Compute Optimizer — rightsizing recommendations for EC2, EBS, Lambda, ECS on Fargate, RDS, Auto Scaling groups.
- AWS Trusted Advisor — cost-optimization checks (idle resources, underutilized instances, unattached EBS, idle load balancers).
- Amazon S3 Storage Lens — organization-wide visibility into S3 usage and activity with cost-saving recommendations.
- S3 Intelligent-Tiering — automatic tier movement between frequent, infrequent, archive, and deep archive access.

**Discounts**
- Savings Plans (Compute, EC2 Instance, SageMaker).
- Reserved Instances for RDS, ElastiCache, OpenSearch, Redshift, DynamoDB, MemoryDB.
- Spot Instances and Spot Fleet/EC2 Fleet for diversified, interruption-tolerant capacity.
- AWS Graviton-based instances for ~20% better price/performance on compatible workloads.

**Governance**
- AWS Organizations — consolidated billing so volume discounts and Savings Plans aggregate across accounts.
- Service Quotas — visibility and control over per-account/Region limits to prevent runaway provisioning.
- Tag Policies and AWS Config — enforce and audit a tagging strategy that powers cost allocation.
- Service Control Policies (SCPs) — block expensive Regions, instance families, or services where not allowed.

**Calculator**
- AWS Pricing Calculator — model architectures and compare On-Demand, SP, and RI costs across Regions before deployment.

## Common Anti-patterns

- Idle or zombie resources left running after projects end (unattached EBS volumes, idle ELBs, stopped EC2 with attached EIPs, dev databases running 24/7).
- Wrong S3 storage class for the access pattern — keeping cold data in Standard instead of Intelligent-Tiering, Glacier IR, or Deep Archive.
- No tagging strategy, so cost cannot be attributed to a team, product, or environment, and showback/chargeback is impossible.
- No rightsizing cadence — instances chosen at launch are never revisited despite Compute Optimizer evidence.
- Ignoring data egress and inter-AZ traffic costs (chatty cross-AZ services, NAT Gateway hairpinning, missing VPC endpoints for S3/DynamoDB).
- Over-provisioned RDS/Aurora (Multi-AZ on dev, oversized instance class, gp2 instead of gp3, long backup retention on non-critical data).
- 100% On-Demand for stable baseline workloads when Savings Plans or RIs would yield 30–70% savings with no architecture change.
- Snowflake architectures and one-off scripts that block migration to managed/serverless services and lock in undifferentiated heavy lifting.

## Review Checklist

1. Is there a named owner or FinOps function accountable for cloud cost optimization?
2. Are budgets and forecasts in place per account/team, with alerts wired to humans who can act?
3. Is Cost Anomaly Detection enabled with notifications routed to engineering and finance?
4. Are cost allocation tags activated, enforced via Tag Policies, and at acceptable coverage (>=95% of spend)?
5. Is the Cost and Usage Report enabled and queryable in Athena or QuickSight?
6. Is the AWS Organizations structure aligned to business units/environments, with SCPs blocking unused Regions and high-cost instance families?
7. Are Savings Plans and Reserved Instance coverage and utilization reviewed monthly at the management-account level?
8. Are Spot or Graviton instances used wherever workload characteristics allow?
9. Is Compute Optimizer reviewed on a recurring cadence and rightsizing actioned within an SLA?
10. Are Auto Scaling, scheduled scaling, or serverless used for variable workloads instead of fixed-size fleets?
11. Is non-production shut down outside business hours (instance scheduler, EventBridge)?
12. Is S3 Intelligent-Tiering or lifecycle policies applied to all large buckets, and are incomplete multipart uploads cleaned up?
13. Are VPC endpoints, CloudFront, and Region placement used to minimize data transfer charges?
14. Is there a documented decommissioning process, and is it triggered when projects end or owners leave?
15. Is there a regular workload review (at least quarterly) that evaluates new AWS services, features, and price reductions?
